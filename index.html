Literature Summary — Bias in Public Welfare Algorithms
Algorithmic Replication of Historical Bias — Research documents how predictive models trained on historical welfare data systematically perpetuate past discrimination. Eubanks (2018) documented how Allegheny County's child welfare screening tool over-flagged low-income and minority families. O'Neil (2016) coined "Weapons of Math Destruction" to describe opaque high-stakes models that reinforce inequality. Benjamin (2019) showed that race-neutral algorithms often embed racial hierarchy through proxies like ZIP code and income.
Proxy Variables — Income, neighborhood, employment history, and prior benefit use serve as proxies for race even without explicit race features. Studies on Medicaid managed care and SNAP recertification show these proxies systematically disadvantage Black and Hispanic applicants (Richardson et al. 2019; Obermeyer et al. 2019).
Fairness Definitions in Tension — Disparate impact, equalized odds, and predictive parity often cannot be simultaneously satisfied (Chouldechova 2017; Kleinberg et al. 2016). Policy choices about which fairness criterion to optimize involve fundamental value judgments (Dwork et al. 2012).
Mitigation Approaches — IBM AIF360 and Fairlearn provide reweighing, adversarial debiasing, and post-processing methods. Threshold calibration per demographic group can reduce FPR disparity while maintaining accuracy (Hardt et al. 2016). Differential privacy protects sensitive group statistics during public reporting (Dwork 2006).

Model Explainability — SHAP-Style Analysis
Permutation importance (a SHAP-compatible surrogate) identifies which features drive predictions. Disparities in feature importance across demographic groups reveal how the model uses different information for different communities — a key source of hidden bias.
SHAP Framework (Lundberg & Lee 2017): SHapley Additive exPlanations decompose each prediction into individual feature contributions. Here we use permutation importance as an accessible approximation. In production, SHAP TreeExplainer values would be computed per-prediction to explain individual case decisions — critical for due process in welfare administration.
Global Feature Importance (All Groups)

Income dominates predictions (34.6% permutation importance), followed by household size (19.7%) and disability (9.4%). These features correlate with race, creating disparate impact even in a "race-blind" model.

Feature Importance by Race Group

Differences across groups reveal differential reliance. If the model uses different features to evaluate different communities, that raises serious fairness concerns about consistency and legitimacy.

Explaining Disparity Through Proxy Variables
Income as a racial proxy: With a $2,600 mean income gap between Black and White applicants in this dataset (and $1,700 for Hispanic), income-based predictions systematically disadvantage minority groups even when race is excluded as a feature. This is the "proxy discrimination" problem documented in credit scoring (Bartlett et al. 2019) and healthcare risk scoring (Obermeyer et al. 2019).
Household size interaction: Lower income + larger household size (a common minority household characteristic) creates compound disadvantage. The 80% FPL threshold calculation interacts with both variables, and errors in either dimension compound.
SHAP for individual explanations: Each welfare decision should be explainable to the applicant. "Your application was flagged because: income $18,500 (threshold $22,400 for HH=3), no disability indicator" is achievable via SHAP's per-instance decomposition. This supports the right to explanation under GDPR Art. 22 and proposed US algorithmic accountability legislation.
Audit recommendation: Run SHAP explanations quarterly, stratified by race/gender. Track feature importance drift — if income weight increases over time for minority groups relative to majority, investigate for data or concept drift that may re-introduce bias.
Case Study: Welfare Fraud Detection Simulation
Fraud detection is a high-stakes application where algorithmic bias has severe consequences: false positives harm legitimate beneficiaries by unjustly flagging them, disproportionately affecting minority communities. Simulated on 5,000 records with injected racial bias.
Real-world precedent: Arkansas' Medicaid benefit determination algorithm (2016) denied care to hundreds of disabled patients due to software errors, with no explanation provided (Harper v. Gillespie). Michigan's fraud detection system (MiDAS) wrongly accused 40,000 people of unemployment fraud between 2013–2015, with Black claimants disproportionately affected (Caplan 2017). These cases illustrate how biased fraud detection causes material harm.

Differential Privacy — Protected Reporting ε = 2.0 (Laplace mechanism)
Differential Privacy (Dwork 2006): Laplace noise with sensitivity 1.0 and ε=2.0 added to all publicly reported selection rates. This provides (2.0, 0)-differential privacy guarantees, preventing adversarial re-identification of individuals from aggregate statistics — critical when reporting welfare outcomes that could stigmatize communities.

DP noise follows Laplace(0, 1/ε) distribution. At ε=2.0, |noise| < 0.05 with ~90% probability, preserving trend accuracy while preventing exact value disclosure.

Policy Recommendations
1. Mandatory Fairness Audits
Welfare ML systems should undergo quarterly disparate impact audits per EEOC 80% rule, with results published publicly.
2. Explainable Decisions
Every automated denial must include a SHAP-based explanation accessible to the applicant, supporting right to appeal and due process.
3. Differential Privacy in Reporting
Public reporting of subgroup statistics should use ε ≤ 2.0 Laplace mechanism to prevent individual re-identification.
4. Human-in-the-Loop
High-risk decisions (benefit termination, fraud flags) should require human review. Algorithm serves as decision support, not final arbiter.
5. Continuous Monitoring
Deploy fairness dashboards like this one in production. Alert on SPD exceeding ±5pp or DI outside [0.85, 1.15] for any group.
6. Participatory Design
Include affected communities in system design, testing, and oversight. Establish community advisory boards with decision-making authority (Costanza-Chock 2020).
